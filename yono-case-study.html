01 Context

India's largest bank,
redesigned

YONO — You Only Need One — is SBI's unified digital banking platform serving over 500 million account holders. Originally launched in 2017 as a core banking utility, it had grown into a fragmented product that could no longer compete with PhonePe, Google Pay, and fintech aggregators targeting India's next generation of banking customers.

YONO 2.0 was a ground-up rethink. The strategic vision: transform YONO from a banking utility into a lifestyle super-app — a single platform for banking, insurance, investments, loans, UPI, and lifestyle services — targeting 20 crore digital users with a completely new technology stack, unified mobile-web architecture, and omnichannel continuity.

"This is not simply a mobile application. We have rewritten the entire internet banking code. If a customer starts a journey on mobile and cannot complete it, they can walk into a branch and complete the same journey seamlessly."

— C.S. Setty, Chairman, State Bank of India · YONO 2.0 Launch, December 2025

The project operated as a structured three-partner engagement: McKinsey & Company as strategy and UX research partner, TCS Interactive as design and development execution partner, and SBI as primary business and regulatory owner. At peak capacity the design organisation comprised 65 designers — UX, visual, and illustration — working across 110+ product journeys simultaneously.

03 The Problem

Insurance was stuck
in the branch

SBI General's Group Personal Accident Insurance (GPAI) was the bank's highest-volume single insurance product — approximately 4 lakh policies sold monthly, generating ₹28 crore in monthly revenue against a ₹100+ crore annual target. Yet over 74% of those sales were branch-driven, dependent on staff personally convincing customers to purchase. The product was push-dependent, not pull-driven. Auto-renewal rate was approximately 40%, meaning 60% of customers churned annually.

The existing YONO 1.0 journey had just 4 screens and virtually no organic discoverability. No peer bank — not HDFC, not ICICI — had solved this natively on their banking application. SBI had a clear first-mover opportunity. The design challenge was making it real across every channel, within every regulatory constraint, for every stakeholder simultaneously.

Complexity factors

01 Regulatory constraints at every gateConsent flows, payment authentication, and policy issuance all required sequential clearance from SBI's ISO, Risk, and Compliance teams. A design change in one area cascaded through all channel variants simultaneously.
02 11 internal SBI stakeholder teamsDigicomm, CVE BU, SBI General, EIS, IT Ops, KYC, GITC IT YONO DEV, GITC Analytics, ISO, Risk, and Compliance — each with independent review authority and the ability to block progression.
03 Multi-channel design requirementThe same underlying journey had to function coherently across 4 distinct channels: mobile app, web browser, branch portal (staff-facing), and field sales tablets. Each had fundamentally different user contexts, technical constraints, and interaction paradigms.
04 Three-partner coordination overheadEvery significant design decision required simultaneous alignment across McKinsey, TCS Interactive, and SBI. No single partner held unilateral approval authority.
04 Process

8 months.
One frozen journey.

GPAI was selected as the anchor journey for its relative simplicity — one product, fixed premium tiers, a well-understood user base. The strategic intent was to use it to establish design patterns, regulatory-cleared interaction models, and component behaviours that could be replicated with confidence across the entire insurance vertical.

The first iteration delivered complete functional coverage of all requirements from McKinsey's Garage documents and SBI's Business Requirements Specification. What drove subsequent iterations was not execution gaps — it was that stakeholders, seeing the journey rendered visually for the first time, began to understand what further possibilities existed. New functionalities were scoped incrementally across rounds. Granular visual decisions — carousel auto-scroll timing, accordion expand behaviour, sticky CTA scroll dynamics, product card information hierarchy, policy wording display — each required multiple rounds of internal negotiation and UX justification before being formally closed.

Approximately 500–600 screens were designed and iterated across the GPAI purchase and manage journeys combined before reaching frozen status.

05 Key Design Decisions

Three decisions
worth examining

The following document specific design problems, the reasoning applied, and the downstream outcomes. These are the kinds of decisions that are not visible in a screen count but determine whether a product works at scale.

01
The product details page — when removing a feature creates a larger problem
GPAI · App & Web · Product Details Page
+

The insurance landing page presented two CTAs on every product card: Buy Now and Know More. The bank's content operations team requested removal of the FAQ accordion from the product details page — maintaining current FAQs across every insurance product was operationally unsustainable within their CMS workflows.

The request was reasonable. My pushback was not on the removal itself, but on what the removal left unresolved. A user selecting Know More is, by definition, in an uncertain state. They are not ready to purchase. Removing the FAQ without replacing the informational function produces a page that fails its only purpose: converting an uncertain browser into a confident buyer. The user selecting Buy Now bypasses this page entirely — so the page was never designed for them.

Outcome

FAQ section removed as operationally requested. The remaining content — USP carousel, policy benefits accordion, policy wording — was restructured to carry the full informational weight of the removed section. The page was redesigned explicitly around the uncertain user mental model, not the committed purchaser who bypasses it.

02
The insurance landing page — consolidation without fragmentation into category silos
Web · Insurance Landing Page · General Insurance + Life Insurance
+

The web insurance experience existed across three separate pages: an insurance landing page, individual product detail pages, and a banners and promotions page. The business requirement was to consolidate all three into a single unified insurance landing page that served both General Insurance and Life Insurance — a significant information architecture challenge given the product diversity of each vertical.

The initial stakeholder proposal was a simple binary toggle: switch between GI and LI, replace the visible content. I proposed against this model. A toggle-based approach pushes users into a predetermined insurance category on arrival and provides no natural pathway across to the other. It replicates the silo problem digitally. Instead, I designed a persistent top fold — banners auto-scrolling with four quick-action anchors, and a GI/LI selector — that remains constant regardless of selection state. Below the fold, content updates contextually based on the user's selection, with a horizontal tab system for product category browsing within each vertical.

Stakeholder objection: the interaction is too complex to build and too unfamiliar for users. My counter was grounded in existing product precedent — the Relationship Overview section of the YONO web portal already implements an identical contextual-update pattern. Users on the platform already understand and use this behaviour. We were not introducing new interaction paradigms; we were applying a known, validated pattern to a new context.

Outcome

Approved and built as specified. The browsing-panel model increased cross-category product exposure organically, replacing the forced-category-selection model. Behavioural consistency with existing YONO web patterns reduced both user cognitive load and development risk simultaneously.

03
The manage journey — a clear separation between viewing state and action state
App & Web · Policy Details Page + Manage Flows
+

The manage journey encompassed 7 distinct policy actions: renewal, deletion, email policy, document download, password management, policy wordings access, and additional servicing options. The initial functional requirement was to surface all 7 CTAs directly on the policy details page.

The resulting page was attempting to serve two incompatible purposes simultaneously — a policy information display and a policy action hub. A user arriving to review their coverage is confronted with 7 management actions they did not seek. The interaction model was cluttered and the information architecture was misleading. Separation of concerns was the correct solution. Reaching agreement on it was the actual design problem.

My proposal: the policy details page presents only policy information. A single persistent sticky CTA — Manage Policy — anchored at the bottom of the view opens a bottom drawer containing all 7 actions. Secondary, lower-frequency actions (email, download, password) are promoted to a second-level drawer state within the manage flow, keeping the primary action set clean and scannable.

Outcome

Approved and implemented. The pattern performed well enough that it was formally adopted as the mandatory reference standard for every manage journey subsequently built across the insurance vertical — all products, all phases, all channels. The web version applied the same separation logic with a viewport-appropriate layout adaptation. Any manage flow in YONO 2.0 insurance today is structurally derived from this decision.

06 Impact

The patterns
became the product

Because GPAI was the first insurance journey designed and the most thoroughly iterated, it became the de facto pattern library for the entire insurance vertical. The branch portal dashboard work independently became the reference standard for all branch portal journeys across Phase 2 and Phase 3. Both remain active reference points today.

07 Standardisation

The audit
nobody assigned

Towards the close of Phase 2, I initiated a design consistency audit across all 42 delivered journeys. No formal assignment existed for this work. The motivation was straightforward: with 65 designers working in parallel across independent journeys, inconsistencies had accumulated across component usage, interaction language, data field labelling, and visual patterns. Left unaddressed, these would compound significantly when Phase 3 scaled to 110+ journeys.

I reviewed every journey individually. Documented current state versus expected state in a structured Excel audit. Tagged each journey owner against their specific issues. Compiled a consolidated report for senior management with a prioritised remediation plan. Monitored resolution progress to formal closure.

The exercise generated friction. Surfacing design quality issues in other teams' work, at scale, during active Phase 3 delivery was uncomfortable for everyone. The work was completed regardless.

Having reviewed all 42 Phase 2 journeys in detail, I became the only member of the team with comprehensive end-to-end knowledge of the entire product. Today, when any designer needs to know whether a pattern has been solved elsewhere in the product, they come to me first.

09 Reflection

What this project
built in me

The most substantive thing this project developed was not skill in any specific interaction pattern — it was the ability to operate with clarity inside institutional complexity. Knowing how to move a decision through a regulatory approval chain without losing its design intent. Knowing how to make the case for a UX principle to a stakeholder who has never considered UX a constraint on their requirements. Knowing how to maintain system coherence when the system is being built by 65 people simultaneously.

The standardisation exercise was the most direct expression of that. It was not in anyone's project plan. It created interpersonal friction. The product is more consistent because it happened — and I understood the entire product more deeply as a result of doing it.

The context for this work: functioning in a lead capacity while graded as a Designer, inside a three-partner enterprise engagement, coordinating across regulatory gatekeepers and 11 internal stakeholder teams, at a scale of 110+ product journeys.
